# Ajna Center

The Ajna Center is an awareness center located between the [[Head Center]] and the [[Throat Center]] in the [[Human Design System]] BodyGraph. It governs conceptualization, mental processing, and the formation of opinions and beliefs. Biologically associated with the pituitary gland, the prefrontal cortex, and the visual cortex.

## Function

The Ajna takes the inspiration and pressure generated by the Head Center and processes it into concepts, opinions, theories, and frameworks. It is the organ of the mind's analytical function. The Ajna does not generate pressure itself; it processes the pressure it receives from the Head and works to make sense of experience.

The Ajna is an awareness center, not a motor. It cannot drive action directly. Its intelligence is conceptual and mental.

## Defined Ajna

A defined Ajna has a fixed, consistent way of thinking. People with a defined Ajna tend to be certain about their opinions -- sometimes rigidly so. Their mental processing runs through reliable, identifiable patterns. Others may experience a defined Ajna person as consistent in their thinking style but potentially inflexible. Approximately 47 percent of people have a defined Ajna.

## Undefined Ajna

An undefined Ajna processes differently depending on whose mental field is present. Common not-self pattern: pretending to certainty in order to appear consistent or reliable. Because the undefined Ajna thinks in many different ways, it may feel under pressure to hold fixed positions to avoid appearing confused or changeable. This performance of certainty masks genuine cognitive flexibility.

The wisdom of an undefined Ajna is the capacity to think in many different ways, to see multiple perspectives, and to not be rigidly attached to any single framework.

## Ajna and the Mind's Role

In Human Design, both the Head and Ajna are explicitly excluded from decision-making. The mind's proper role is to process, observe, and serve as outer authority for others -- not to steer its own person's life. Ra Uru Hu consistently described the mind as a passenger in the vehicle of the body, here to witness and comment on the life, not to drive it.
